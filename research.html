Work in Progress

Re-industrialising Indonesia: Lessons from China, Capital Intensity, and Labour-Market Realities

Indonesian Quarterly by CSIS Indonesia

Abstract

After decades of premature deindustrialisation, Indonesia’s factories have lost their shine just as its workers seek stable, high‐productivity employment. Manufacturing’s share of GDP peaked too early, leaving a gap between abundant labour and limited opportunities. This paper asks: How can Indonesia reignite its industrial engine? We look to China’s journey from “Made in Just‐The‐Basics” to “Made in the World” for clues, while respecting Indonesia’s own capital‐intensity paradox and labour‐market realities. By combining Dependency Theory’s warning against over‐reliance on outsiders, Global Value Chain insights on export‐led upgrading, and Lin’s New Structural Economics on state‐guided growth, we build a framework (Diagram A) that steers our analysis. Through side‐by‐side storytelling of China’s policy milestones (1978–2022) and Indonesia’s shifts from Sukarno to Prabowo, we uncover the recipe behind China’s success: starting with labour‐heavy manufacturing, layering on infrastructure, and then deepening capital investment. We then explore which ingredients might work in Indonesia—suggesting focused, labour‐absorbing clusters, targeted infrastructure support, and a gradual uptick in capital intensity. Finally, we reflect on hurdles like governance, scale, and energy needs, offering broader lessons for any nation seeking to revive its factories.

City Solar Mandates and Paradiplomacy: A Difference-in-Differences Analysis of Five ASEAN Capitals

Journal of Paradiplomacy and City Networks

Abstract

ASEAN’s pledge to raise the share of renewables in its primary energy mix to 23% by 2025 has given metropolitan governments a prominent role in accelerating solar deployment. Yet, installed rooftop photovoltaic (PV) capacity remains highly uneven: Singapore exceeded 1.17 GWp by Q4 2023, on the back of its SolarNova procurement scheme, while Bangkok and Kuala Lumpur only began dedicated city programs in 2024. This article leverages a newly compiled five-city panel (2010-2024) of annual rooftop solar capacity, financial indicators, and policy adoption dates to test, using a multi-period difference-in-differences design, whether city-level rooftop solar rules causally increase per-capita PV uptake. Results show that passing a rooftop-solar mandate or quota lifts annual installed capacity by 0.25 MWp per 100,000 residents (p < 0.05) within two years, after controlling for funding flows, electricity tariffs, and GDP per capita. Event-study plots confirm parallel pre-trends, and estimates are robust to Wild-cluster bootstrap errors and the exclusion of early-mover Singapore. The findings underscore the paradiplomatic leverage of city governments: tailored regulations and procurement tools can materially accelerate local energy transitions even in the absence of generous national subsidies.

Commercial diplomacy in times of crisis: Lessons from Indonesia’s coffee exports to Egypt

Kasetsart Journal of Social Sciences

Abstract

This study examines the strategic role of commercial diplomacy in enhancing Indonesia’s coffee exports to Egypt. Using a commercial diplomacy framework, the research explores how six key components—goal setting, stakeholder engagement, trade promotion, market intelligence and negotiations, advocacy and public diplomacy, and dispute resolution—have shaped Indonesia’s trade strategy. The findings highlight the effectiveness of these efforts in overcoming market barriers, fostering long-term economic partnerships, and ensuring sustained export growth. Furthermore, the study underscores the significance of structured diplomatic interventions in maintaining Indonesia’s export competitiveness and offers insights for policymakers aiming to strengthen international trade networks through targeted economic diplomacy.
